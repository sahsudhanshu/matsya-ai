# Indo-Pacific Tarpon (*Megalops cyprinoides*)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

Scientific Name: *Megalops cyprinoides* (Broussonet, 1782)[^1][^2]

Family: Megalopidae (tarpons)[^3][^1]

Global Common Names: Indo-Pacific tarpon, Indo-Pacific tarpon (oxeye), oxeye herring, oxeye, tarpon, Indo-Pacific oxeye.[^4][^5][^6][^1]

Regional & Local Names:
- South Asia
  - Bangladesh/Bangla: Nanchil koral (নানচিল কোরাল).[^3]
  - India/Oriya: Punnikowa, Vorsa.[^5]
  - India/Marathi: Vadas.[^5]
  - India/Malayalam: Valathan, Velathan, വാളതാന്.[^5]
  - India/Tamil: Murankendai (முரன்கெண்டை) and related spellings.[^5]
- Southeast Asia
  - Indonesia (Bahasa Indonesia, Javanese): Trupang (local vernacular for this tarpon).[^5]
  - Indonesia/Aceh: Ikan bulan.[^5]
  - Philippines/Cebuano: Salongsong.[^5]
  - Philippines/Hiligaynon: Tewal.[^5]
  - Vietnam/Vietnamese: Cá Cháo Lớn.[^7]
- East Asia & Pacific
  - Japan/Japanese: イセゴイ (Isegoi).[^7][^5]
  - Papua New Guinea/Motu: U-u-la.[^5]
- Europe & Other
  - French: tarpon indo-pacifique, tarpon.[^7]
  - Spanish: tarpón indo-pacífico.[^7]

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)

Primary Identifying Features:
- Elongated, moderately deep, laterally compressed body with very large cycloid scales (armor-like sheen). Scales along the lateral line typically 35–42.[^1]
- Large superior (upturned) mouth with a protrusible lower jaw and a prominent bony plate in the lower jaw—a key tarpon trait.[^4][^1]
- Large eyes with a noticeable adipose eyelid.
- Single dorsal fin placed mid-body; last dorsal ray extended as a long filament often trailing behind the fish.[^4][^1]
- Deeply forked caudal fin; relatively small pelvic fins set behind the dorsal fin origin.[^1]

Coloration & Markings (with CV-oriented detail):
- Dorsal (back): Blue-green to olive. Approximate hex: #2F4F4F–#3B5A4A.[^1]
- Flanks: Bright, mirror-like silver, often reflecting ambient hues; hex around #C0C0C0–#E0E0E0.
- Belly: Silvery white; hex around #F5F5F5.
- Fins: Generally hyaline to dusky; edges of dorsal and anal fins often blackish, especially in larger fish.[^1]
- No strong vertical bars or spots; body appears clean-silver with subtle darker dorsal shading.
- Distinctive long filament from dorsal fin tip is a strong shape-based marker in lateral imagery.[^4]

Sexual Dimorphism:
- Externally minimal; males and females very similar in coloration and shape.[^1]
- Large, very heavy-bodied individuals are often females (based on general tarpon biology), but this is not a reliable field cue and usually requires dissection or gonad examination.

Size Metrics:
- Common length: 30–60 cm.[^4]
- Maximum reported: Around 120–150 cm and up to approximately 18 kg.[^4][^1]
- Juveniles encountered in estuaries can be 10–30 cm; micro-tarpon used in sport fisheries can be 30–80 cm.[^8][^4]
- Many jurisdictions do not manage this species with specific size limits; where tarpon regulations exist, they are often generic (e.g., catch-and-release only) and must be checked locally.

Similar Species Look-alikes:
- Atlantic tarpon (*Megalops atlanticus*): Similar silver herring-like body but confined to Atlantic; Indo-Pacific tarpon has Indo-Pacific distribution. Differentiation by geography is usually sufficient; detailed meristics require expert work.[^8][^4]
- Large herrings and shads (e.g., *Tenualosa* spp.): Lack the extended dorsal filament and have different mouth placement (typically terminal or subterminal) and smaller scales.[^8][^1]
- Milkfish (*Chanos chanos*): More fusiform body, terminal mouth, and absence of dorsal filament; dorsal fin positioned slightly differently.

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters

Water Type:
- Euryhaline species inhabiting marine, brackish, and freshwater systems.[^9][^4][^1]
- Frequently amphidromous, moving between coastal marine waters, estuaries, and rivers.[^8][^4]
- Tolerates low dissolved oxygen by air-gulping into a lung-like swim bladder.[^4]

Geographic Distribution:
- Broad Indo-West Pacific distribution from the Red Sea and East Africa through the Indian Ocean to Southeast Asia, northern Australia, and west Pacific islands (e.g., Marianas).[^6][^4]
- Recorded from coastal Bangladesh and India (Bay of Bengal), including Hooghly estuary, Chilka Lake, and Meghna River.[^3][^1]
- Found inland in large lakes and reservoirs; confirmed far inland such as Lake Malawi in Africa.[^8][^4]

Micro-Habitats:
- Coastal: Shallow inshore waters, surf zones, and reef edges.[^9]
- Estuarine: Mangrove-fringed creeks, tidal rivers, brackish lagoons, floodplains, and canals; juveniles especially concentrated in bays and estuaries.[^9][^8][^1]
- Freshwater: Lower to mid-reaches of large rivers, floodplain lakes, and reservoirs; in Papua New Guinea, can be found under dense mats of floating vegetation such as *Salvinia molesta*.[^9]

Depth Profile:
- Recorded to depths of about 50 m but most commonly very shallow and surface-oriented in inshore and estuarine waters.[^9]
- Frequently roll or gulp at the surface, especially in low-oxygen backwaters and during calm, hot periods.[^4]
- Juveniles stay in very shallow, sheltered margins of estuaries and creeks; adults more often in open coastal or deep estuarine channels.[^8][^1]

Optimal Water Conditions (where documented or inferred from habitat):
- Temperature: Occupies tropical and subtropical waters; commonly found in warm waters consistent with Indo-Pacific coastal and estuarine systems, typically in the mid-20s °C range.[^6][^4]
- Salinity: From freshwater (0 ppt) to full-strength seawater; often in mesohaline estuaries.[^9][^1][^4]
- pH: Estuarine and coastal habitats typically near-neutral to slightly alkaline; published exact pH ranges for this species are scarce, but they occupy standard coastal and riverine pH regimes.
- Dissolved oxygen: Tolerant of hypoxic conditions due to specialized air-breathing swim bladder; often present in warm, oxygen-poor lagoons where other fish avoid.[^4]
- Water clarity: Occurs in both clear and turbid waters; juveniles often in turbid mangrove creeks and floodplains where visual predators are reduced.[^8][^1]

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior

Natural Diet:
- Larvae (leptocephalus stage): Transparent, planktonic, feeding on particulate organic matter and small plankton, similar to eel leptocephali.[^8][^4]
- Juveniles: Small fishes, shrimps, and aquatic invertebrates taken in estuaries and lower rivers.[^1][^8]
- Adults: Predatory; feed mainly on small schooling fishes and crustaceans in inshore and estuarine waters.[^8][^4]

Feeding Behavior:
- Primarily an active predator that also uses opportunistic ambush in mangrove and structure-rich habitats.[^9][^8]
- Frequently hunts near the surface, often associated with baitfish schools; known to strike fast, with strong, surface-oriented runs when hooked.[^8][^4]

Peak Activity Windows:
- Many estuarine sport reports indicate peaks at dawn and dusk when baitfish are concentrated near the surface; tidal movement improves feeding as currents bring forage into creeks and drains.[^10]
- Low-light conditions (overcast days, turbid water) often see increased rolling and feeding activity.
- Being air-breathers, they may be particularly conspicuous and catchable during hot, low-oxygen periods as they surface frequently to gulp air.[^4]

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear

Best Live/Natural Baits:
- Small live fishes common in local estuaries (e.g., mullet, anchovies, small herrings) rigged on light wire or fluorocarbon leaders; baits presented near the surface or mid-water.[^11][^10]
- Live or cut prawns and shrimps drifted along mangrove edges or creek mouths.
- In some regions, small live crabs and other crustaceans are effective around mangroves and rocky shorelines.[^11]

Best Artificial Lures:
- Small to medium spoons and metal slices worked quickly near the surface or just sub-surface; widely used by local anglers in estuaries.[^10]
- Soft-plastic grubs and swimbaits on light jig heads retrieved with a steady or twitch-pause cadence; particularly effective for schooling "micro-tarpon".[^10]
- Small surface poppers and stickbaits mimicking injured baitfish; topwater strikes can be spectacular in low-light periods.[^12][^11]

Recommended Tackle:
- Rod: 6’6”–8’ medium to medium-heavy spinning or baitcasting rods for estuarine fish to ~5 kg; heavier inshore rods for larger individuals.
- Reel: 3000–5000 size spinning reels or equivalent baitcasters with smooth drags, spooled with PE 1–2 (10–25 lb) braid for small to mid-sized fish.
- Main line: 10–30 lb braid depending on structure and fish size.
- Leader: 20–40 lb fluorocarbon to handle abrasive gill plates and hard jaws.[^11]
- Hooks: Strong, thin-gauge singles or small trebles; circle hooks with live bait improve corner-of-mouth hookups and release survival.

Community & Regional Techniques:
- Southeast Asian estuaries: Shore and small-boat anglers cast small spoons and soft plastics to rolling fish at creek mouths and mangrove points on moving tides.[^10]
- South Asia (Bangladesh/India): Often taken incidentally in gill nets, cast nets, and traditional estuarine traps targeting mullets and prawns; dedicated rod anglers focus on surface-rolling fish near flood tides.[^3][^1]
- Pacific islands: Juveniles sometimes targeted with small hooks and live-bait or dough-bait rigs around floodplains and irrigation canals, especially by subsistence fishers.

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations

Handling Warnings:
- No venomous spines; main risk is from sharp gill plates, hard bony mouth, and large hooks.[^4]
- Large, thrashing fish can cause hook-related injuries; use lip-grips with caution and support body weight horizontally.

Catch & Release Best Practices:
- Use barbless or de-barbed hooks where possible and fight fish quickly to reduce exhaustion.
- Keep fish in the water whenever feasible; if lifting for photos, support under the belly and avoid bending the spine.[^4]
- Revive by holding the fish facing into current until strong kicks indicate recovery; air-breathing ability helps but does not eliminate stress from prolonged air exposure.

Conservation Status & General Regulations:
- Often not individually assessed; in some regional lists categorized as Data Deficient.[^3]
- In many areas, tarpons are primarily sportfish with a strong catch-and-release culture; harvest may be limited or culturally uncommon.[^4]
- Local regulations (if any) may include gear restrictions and protected nursery zones in estuaries—anglers must verify current local laws.

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile

Commercial Value:
- Generally considered low-value as table fish due to numerous intermuscular bones and relatively poor fillet yield.[^4]
- More important as a sportfish and occasionally as a minor component in estuarine mixed catches.

Meat Quality:
- Flesh described as bony and of modest eating quality unless carefully prepared; bones can be problematic for consumers.[^4]
- Where consumed, often cooked in ways that soften or work around the many bones.

Regional Preparation:
- In parts of South and Southeast Asia, smaller fish may be eaten fried or curried after extensive deboning or pressure-cooking.
- In some communities, tarpon may be dried or smoked to preserve seasonal catches, with bones tolerated or removed during flaking.

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)

Q1: How do I target Indo-Pacific tarpon rolling in a shallow mangrove creek at dawn?
A: Position down-current of the rolling fish and cast small spoons or 2–3 inch soft-plastic grubs slightly beyond them, then retrieve at a moderate speed just under the surface, using 20–30 lb fluorocarbon leader to prevent cut-offs on their gill plates.[^11][^10]

Q2: Can Indo-Pacific tarpon live in pure freshwater impoundments, or do they need some salinity?
A: They are fully euryhaline and occur naturally in freshwater rivers and lakes as well as estuaries, so they can persist in pure freshwater impoundments provided temperatures are tropical and there is access to suitable forage.[^9][^1][^4]

Q3: What kind of bait should I bring if I am fishing an estuary where locals say tarpon are present but I do not know the forage?
A: Bring small live baits like mullet, sardines, or shrimps and rig them under a float or on a freelined setup; these generalist baits match many Indo-Pacific tarpon prey types and are widely successful across their range.[^11]

Q4: Are Indo-Pacific tarpon safe to handle with bare hands when unhooking beside a kayak?
A: They lack venom, but their sharp gill plates and large hooks can easily cut you, so support them under the belly and grip the jaw carefully or use a lip-grip, keeping the fish in the water and controlling head shakes before removing the hook.[^4]

Q5: Why do Indo-Pacific tarpon keep gulping at the surface in a hot, stagnant backwater, and does that affect how I should fish for them?
A: They are using their modified swim bladder to gulp air and tolerate low dissolved oxygen, which makes them more visible; targeting rolling fish with surface or shallow presentations is particularly effective in those hypoxic conditions.[^10][^4]

# Jaguar Guapote (*Parachromis managuensis*)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

Scientific Name: *Parachromis managuensis* (Günther, 1867).[^13][^14]

Family: Cichlidae (cichlids).[^15][^13]

Global Common Names:
- English: Jaguar guapote, jaguar cichlid, Aztec cichlid, spotted guapote, tiger guapote, Managua cichlid, managuense cichlid.[^16][^14][^17][^13]
- Spanish: Guapote tigre, guapote barcino, mojarra de Managua, pinta.[^14][^17][^13][^16]

Regional & Local Names:
- Central America
  - Costa Rica/Nicaragua (Spanish): Guapote tigre, guapote barcino, mojarra de Managua.[^17][^13][^16]
- North America
  - USA (especially Florida)/English: Jaguar guapote, jaguar cichlid, Aztec cichlid, tiger guapote.[^15][^14][^17]
- Europe & Other
  - Germany/German: Managua-Buntbarsch.[^16][^17]
  - Sweden/Swedish: Jaguarcichlid.[^17]
  - Denmark/Danish: Jaguarcichlide.[^17]
  - Estonia/Estonian: Jaaguar-keiserahven, Managua parakroom.[^17]
  - Brazil/Portuguese: Peixe-jaguar.[^17]
  - China/Mandarin Chinese: 马拉丽体鱼 (Ma la li ti yu).[^17]
- Aquarium trade in Philippines and elsewhere: Managuense (English usage in Philippines aquarium context).cite:38]

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)

Primary Identifying Features:
- Robust, elongate-ovoid body moderately compressed laterally.[^18][^19][^13]
- Large oblique mouth with prominent lower jaw extending beyond upper; visible canine teeth, giving a predatory profile.[^19][^13][^15]
- Dorsal fin long and continuous, with spiny anterior section and softer posterior rays; males show extended dorsal fin filaments.[^13]
- Anal fin with 7–9 spines; caudal fin rounded.[^19][^13]

Coloration & Markings (CV cues):
- Base body color silvery or golden-green to purplish on sides, darker moss-green dorsally, whitish to yellowish belly.[^13]
- Numerous black blotches and spots over flanks and fins; along the lateral-line area a row of several large, irregular black blotches forms the namesake "jaguar" pattern.[^19][^13]
- Distinct black stripe from eye to the edge of operculum, often appearing as two broken streaks above and below.[^13][^19]
- Fins (except pectorals) generally gray to black with heavy black spotting or marbling; pectorals often dusky yellow.[^19]
- Approximate color hexes: base silvery-gold #C2B280–#D9CBA3, dark blotches #1A1A1A, dorsal green-brown #3C4A2E.

Sexual Dimorphism:
- Males larger, reaching up to ~65 cm total length; females to ~40 cm.[^13]
- Males typically show more elongated dorsal and anal fin extensions; females often rounder-bodied, especially when ripe with eggs.[^13]

Size Metrics:
- Average adult in natural habitats: around 30–45 cm; many aquarium specimens in 25–35 cm range.[^18]
- Maximum reported: up to 55–65 cm.[^18][^13]
- Weight data are variable; large individuals can reach several kilograms.

Similar Species Look-alikes:
- Other large *Parachromis* species (e.g., *P. dovii*): *P. dovii* (wolf cichlid) typically more uniform golden background with fewer discrete jaguar-like spots; head profile and spotting patterns differ and may require expert comparison.
- Larger Central American cichlids (e.g., *Amphilophus* spp.): Often lack the distinct eye-to-operculum black band and tight jaguar-like black spotting, instead showing bars or mottling.

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters

Water Type:
- Freshwater species inhabiting lakes, reservoirs, and slow rivers.[^18][^13]
- Highly tolerant of eutrophic (nutrient-rich) and hypoxic conditions; often persists where more sensitive fishes decline.[^13]

Geographic Distribution:
- Native: Atlantic slope of Central America from the Ulúa River in Honduras south to Matina River basin in Costa Rica.[^13]
- Introduced: Established in Florida and Puerto Rico; locally established or reported in Hawaii, Nevada, southern Louisiana, and various other tropical/subtropical locales via aquarium releases.[^20][^15]

Micro-Habitats:
- Prefers turbid lakes and lowland reservoirs with mud bottoms, often in warm, eutrophic waters.[^13]
- Also found in ponds, roadside ditches, canals, and warm creeks where visibility may be low and vegetation or woody cover is present.[^15][^18][^19]
- Often near bottom or mid-depth, where it can ambush prey and excavate substrate for nesting.[^18]

Depth Profile:
- Typically a mid-water to near-bottom resident within relatively shallow lakes and canals; often encountered from shoreline margins to several meters depth.
- During breeding, pairs defend nests on open substrate or slight depressions in shallow littoral zones.[^18]

Optimal Water Conditions (from field and aquarium data):
- Temperature: 24–36 °C; thrives particularly in warm water near upper tropical ranges.[^18][^13]
- pH: 7.0–8.7 (neutral to moderately alkaline).[^18][^13]
- Hardness: 10–15 dGH (moderately hard).[^18][^13]
- Dissolved oxygen: Tolerant of low oxygen, commonly present in hypoxic, eutrophic systems.[^13]
- Water clarity: Prefers turbid environments; often associated with suspended sediments and algae blooms.[^13]

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior

Natural Diet:
- Juveniles: Macroinvertebrates, aquatic insect larvae, and small crustaceans.[^13]
- Adults: Primarily piscivorous; feed heavily on small fishes and other vertebrates where available.[^18][^13]

Feeding Behavior:
- Aggressive ambush and stalk predator; uses cover and turbidity to close distance before rapid strike.[^18]
- Territorial, especially around nesting sites; will defend feeding territories against conspecifics and other large fishes.[^18]

Peak Activity Windows:
- Generally diurnal with peak feeding often around low-light periods (early morning and late afternoon) when prey fishes are more vulnerable.[^18]
- Warm water increases activity; sudden cold snaps can reduce movement and feeding, particularly at the species’ lower lethal temperature (~12 °C).[^15]

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear

Best Live/Natural Baits:
- Live minnows or small cichlids native to the water body, hooked through the nose or back on strong single hooks.
- Large earthworms or shrimp pieces can also be effective for smaller individuals.

Best Artificial Lures:
- Medium-sized crankbaits, jerkbaits, and swimbaits in baitfish color patterns (silver, gold, perch-like) worked near structure.
- Soft-plastic jerk shads or paddle-tails dragged or hopped close to the bottom or past ambush points.
- Spinnerbaits and jigs around submerged timber, rock piles, and drop-offs.

Recommended Tackle:
- Rod: 7–8 ft medium-heavy to heavy baitcasting or spinning rods to handle powerful runs and heavy cover.
- Reel: Quality baitcaster or 4000–5000 size spinning reel with strong drag.
- Main line: 30–50 lb braid or 15–25 lb monofilament, depending on cover density.
- Leader: 20–40 lb fluorocarbon or mono leader; wire leader rarely needed but can help against abrasion from teeth.

Community & Regional Techniques:
- Central American lakes: Local anglers often fish with live tilapia or native minnows drifted or slow-trolled near weedlines and submerged logs; bottom-set bait rigs near steep banks are common.
- Florida canals: Anglers target them similar to largemouth bass—casting spinnerbaits, swimbaits, and jigs into culverts, bridge pilings, and canal intersections.
- Subsistence fisheries: In native range, frequently caught with gill nets, cast nets, and baited longlines, especially in shallow littoral zones.

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations

Handling Warnings:
- No venom, but strong jaws and sharp teeth can inflict serious bites—avoid putting fingers near mouth.
- Dorsal and anal fin spines are sharp; handle with wet hands behind the head and supporting the belly.

Catch & Release Best Practices:
- Use heavy gear to shorten fight time and reduce exhaustion.
- Land fish quickly, keep them in the water as much as possible, and support horizontally when lifting.
- De-barb hooks where legal to minimize handling time; release away from nesting sites during breeding season to reduce reproductive disruption.

Conservation Status & General Regulations:
- IUCN global status: Least Concern.[^18]
- In native range, valued as both food and sport fish; local regulations vary but often allow harvest.
- In many introduced regions (e.g., Florida, Puerto Rico), considered non-native or invasive; regulations may encourage harvest and discourage release—check specific regional rules.[^15]

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile

Commercial Value:
- Regionally important food fish in parts of Central America; also traded in international aquarium market as a large show cichlid.[^13]

Meat Quality:
- Firm, white flesh typical of large cichlids; generally regarded as good eating where consumed.
- Moderate bone structure, with a central spine and rib bones manageable by experienced fish processors.

Regional Preparation:
- Central America: Commonly fried, grilled, or used in stews; larger fish often filleted and pan-fried.
- In some communities, may be cooked whole with scales removed and deep-fried for shared meals.

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)

Q1: What kind of structure should I focus on when targeting jaguar guapote in a turbid Central American lake?
A: Work mud-bottom points, submerged timber, and rocky drop-offs in 2–6 m of water, casting swimbaits or live baits tight to cover where these territorial predators hold and ambush smaller fish.[^13][^18]

Q2: How warm does the water need to be before jaguar guapote start feeding aggressively?
A: They are tropical cichlids that prefer 24–30 °C and remain active even up to about 36 °C, but activity drops sharply when temperatures approach their lower limit near 12 °C.[^15][^13][^18]

Q3: Are jaguar guapote safe to release in Florida canals, or should I keep them if legal?
A: In Florida they are non-native and considered potentially invasive, so regulations and conservation perspectives generally favor removing them when legal rather than releasing them back; always confirm current local rules.[^15]

Q4: What size line and leader should I use around heavy cover where jaguar guapote are known to nest?
A: Use 30–50 lb braid with a 25–40 lb fluorocarbon leader to pull fish out of timber and rocks while resisting abrasion from their teeth and rough mouths.

Q5: Will jaguar guapote respond to topwater lures, or should I stay subsurface?
A: They can hit topwaters in low light over shallow structure, but more consistent results usually come from subsurface crankbaits, swimbaits, and jigs worked at mid-depth or near the bottom where they patrol and stage.[^18]

# Janitor Fish (*Pterygoplichthys* spp., esp. *Pterygoplichthys disjunctivus* / *P. pardalis*)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

Scientific Name: Primarily invasive sailfin armored catfishes of the genus *Pterygoplichthys* (family Loricariidae), especially *Pterygoplichthys disjunctivus* and *P. pardalis* in the Philippines and many Asian waters.[^21][^22][^23][^24]

Family: Loricariidae (armored catfishes, suckermouth catfishes).[^22][^23]

Global Common Names:
- English: Janitor fish, sailfin catfish, sailfin pleco, suckermouth armored catfish, Amazon sailfin catfish, pleco.[^23][^25][^26][^22]

Regional & Local Names:
- Philippines (Tagalog/English usage): Janitor fish.[^25][^26][^23]
- Philippines (general English trade): Janitor fish (ornamental trade name).[^26]
- Mexico/Spanish: Various sailfin catfish common names when established locally; specific regional names vary.[^22]
- India and other Asian countries: Often referred to generically as "pleco" or "sucker catfish" in aquarium and aquaculture contexts; published vernacular names are sparse.[^22]

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)

Primary Identifying Features:
- Medium to large armored catfish (commonly 30–55 cm, sometimes >70 cm), with heavy bony plates covering the body instead of scales.[^22]
- Ventral, suckermouth-type mouth forming a round to oval suction disk enabling attachment to hard surfaces.[^22]
- Tall, sail-like dorsal fin with 9–14 rays, often extended when alarmed or at rest in open water.[^22]
- Depressed head with small eyes set dorsally; pectoral and pelvic fins strong and spiny.

Coloration & Markings (CV cues):
- Base coloration generally dark brown to grayish-black with lighter vermiculated (worm-like) or spotted patterns, varying by species.[^22]
  - For *P. disjunctivus*: Dense vermiculated dark pattern over lighter background; pattern extends across dorsal and caudal fins ("vermiculated sailfin catfish").[^24][^21]
  - For *P. pardalis*: More distinct leopard-like spots on body and fins.
- Belly usually lighter but still mottled; color hexes approximate: dark plate #2B1B13–#3B2A20, lighter interspaces #7A6248–#A0835A.
- Overall silhouette: elongated body with large sail dorsal and heavy armored tail, easily distinguished from native smooth-skinned catfishes.

Sexual Dimorphism:
- External differences subtle; some populations show slightly broader, more muscular heads and pectorals in males versus fuller body profile in gravid females.
- Males may guard nests and exhibit more intense territorial behavior in burrows.[^24][^22]

Size Metrics:
- Common length range: 30–55 cm.[^22]
- Maximum length: Can exceed 70 cm in favorable conditions.[^22]
- In invaded Philippine wetlands, many captured individuals are within 20–40 cm, with some much larger trophy specimens reported.[^21][^24]

Similar Species Look-alikes:
- Native loricariid-like fishes are generally absent in invaded regions such as the Philippines; confusion may occur among different *Pterygoplichthys* species, which require detailed counts and pattern analysis for accurate species ID.[^21][^22]
- Easily distinguished from native soft-skinned catfish (e.g., *Clarias*, *Pangasianodon*) by heavy bony armor, large sailfin dorsal, and ventral suckermouth.

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters

Water Type:
- Freshwater systems: rivers, lakes, reservoirs, marshes, and canals.[^24][^21][^22]

Geographic Distribution:
- Native: Tropical South America.[^22]
- Introduced/invasive: Widely established in Mexico, Puerto Rico, India, the Philippines, Taiwan, Panama, Trinidad, Guyana, Japan, Peru, Singapore, Sumatra, Malaysia, Java, Hawaii, Florida, Texas, and other regions via aquarium releases and escapes.[^22]
- In the Philippines, heavily established in Laguna de Bay, Marikina and Pasig Rivers, and Agusan Marsh region (Mindanao) among many other freshwater bodies.[^27][^21][^24]

Micro-Habitats:
- Occupy lakes, rivers, and creeks, often concentrating near banks where they burrow and graze.[^21][^24]
- Prefer soft banks (mud or clay) in slow-flowing or still waters where they excavate spawning burrows.[^24][^22]
- Frequently attach to submerged wood, rocks, and artificial structures, grazing biofilm and algae.

Depth Profile:
- Common in shallow littoral zones from shoreline to a few meters depth, particularly near vegetated or undercut banks.
- Can occupy deeper channels in rivers and lakes but still strongly associated with bottom and bank structures.

Optimal Water Conditions:
- Temperature: Tropical freshwater; exact ranges not always specified, but established in warm lowland waters of tropical and subtropical regions.[^22]
- pH and hardness: Tolerant of a wide range of conditions, from slightly acidic to alkaline and soft to hard water, as evidenced by presence across diverse invaded waters.[^22]
- Dissolved oxygen: Highly tolerant; adults can surface-gulp and withstand degraded, silted, and polluted habitats better than many natives.[^22]
- Water clarity: Often abundant in turbid, silt-laden systems where their burrowing increases turbidity further and destabilizes banks.[^28][^22]

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior

Natural Diet:
- Primarily periphyton and detritus: Scrapes algae, biofilm, and organic matter from submerged surfaces using rasping mouthparts.[^22]
- Also consumes plant debris, small invertebrates, and sediment-associated organic material.

Feeding Behavior:
- Bottom-oriented grazer; remains attached to rocks, wood, and artificial substrates while feeding.
- Nocturnal and crepuscular activity common; often rests immobile during brighter daylight.

Peak Activity Windows:
- Most active at night and during low light, especially in clear or shallow waters.
- In turbid systems may feed extended hours; feeding peaks often coincide with cooler nighttime temperatures.

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear

Best Live/Natural Baits:
- Because janitor fish are primarily algal and detrital grazers, traditional animal baits are less effective.
- In Philippine recreational fishing reports, effective baits include algae-coated rocks employed as attractant and dough baits made from rice bran ("masa"), cornmeal, or bread mixed with flavorings, pressed around small hooks to be rasped by feeding fish.[^25]

Best Artificial Lures:
- Conventional artificial lures are generally ineffective due to grazing feeding mode.
- Some anglers snag or foul-hook them inadvertently when using bottom lures for other species.

Recommended Tackle:
- Rod: Medium or medium-heavy spinning rod sufficient to control 30–50 cm armored fish.
- Reel: 2500–4000 size spinning reel.
- Main line: 10–20 lb monofilament or braid.
- Terminal tackle: Small but strong hooks (e.g., size 4–1/0) embedded in dough bait; some use simple bottom rigs with small sinkers to keep bait in contact with substrate.

Community & Regional Techniques:
- Philippines small-scale fisheries: Fishers use gill nets, fish traps, and hook-and-line; janitor fish often caught incidentally or targeted where they are abundant and damage gear.[^21][^24]
- In some localities, angling for janitor fish involves chumming with rice bran-based mixtures and presenting dough-bait on the bottom, as described by sport anglers documenting catches.[^25]
- Government and community efforts sometimes encourage physical removal campaigns (bounty or buy-back programs) where captured janitor fish are processed for by-products (e.g., animal feed, fertilizer) rather than human food.[^28]

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations

Handling Warnings:
- Strong pectoral and dorsal fin spines can puncture hands; bony plates are abrasive.
- Handle with gloves or by grasping at the base of tail while supporting body, avoiding dorsal and pectoral spines.

Catch & Release Best Practices:
- In most invaded regions, catch-and-release is discouraged; management aims for removal due to ecological impacts.[^24][^21][^22]
- If release is mandated (e.g., in some ornamental contexts), minimize air exposure and return fish gently to reduce injury.

Conservation Status & General Regulations:
- IUCN GISD profile treats *Pterygoplichthys* spp. as invasive alien species with significant ecological and economic impacts.[^22]
- Many jurisdictions classify janitor fish as invasive; regulations may prohibit release, encourage culling, or restrict import and trade; anglers should verify local invasive species policies.

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile

Commercial Value:
- Low to negligible as a human food fish in most invaded regions; high bony armor and low flesh yield reduce culinary interest.[^28][^22]
- Investigated for use as raw material for fishmeal, animal feed, fertilizer, and other by-products in the Philippines and elsewhere.[^28]

Meat Quality:
- Edible flesh is reportedly mild but difficult to access due to thick bony armor and numerous small bones.
- Processing labor often outweighs value, limiting broad acceptance as food.

Regional Preparation:
- In some experimental or subsistence contexts, janitor fish are filleted or stewed after heavy cleaning and removal of armor; not widely documented as traditional cuisine.
- More commonly processed industrially (e.g., cooked, dried, and ground) for use in livestock or aquaculture feeds.[^28]

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)

Q1: What is the most effective way for a bank angler in the Philippines to actually hook janitor fish instead of just seeing them stuck to rocks?
A: Use a bottom rig with a small strong hook completely buried in a rice-bran-based dough bait or algae-rich paste placed directly on the substrate where janitor fish graze, and wait for them to rasp the bait until the hook sets.[^25]

Q2: Can I safely release janitor fish if I catch them while targeting native tilapia?
A: In most invaded areas, management agencies discourage releasing janitor fish because of their serious impacts on banks, water quality, and native biodiversity; where legal, it is usually better to humanely dispatch and dispose of or donate them for by-product use.[^21][^24][^22]

Q3: How do janitor fish affect my fishing for other species in a marsh?
A: Their burrowing undermines banks and increases turbidity, which can alter habitats for native fishes and invertebrates, potentially reducing catches of native species and damaging nets and other gear they collide with or entangle.[^28][^21][^22]

Q4: What tackle changes should I make if janitor fish keep breaking light lines when hooked on my dough-bait rig?
A: Upsize to 12–20 lb main line and use slightly larger, stronger hooks; keep drag relatively tight so armored fish cannot bury into snags or burrows.

Q5: Do janitor fish bite at particular times of day?
A: They are often more active and willing to feed in low light and at night, but in turbid, nutrient-rich waters they may graze much of the day; focus dough-bait efforts in early morning and evening for best results.

# Knifefish (*Chitala ornata* – Clown Knifefish / Clown Featherback)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature

Scientific Name: *Chitala ornata* (Gray, 1831).[^29][^30][^31]

Family: Notopteridae (featherbacks or knifefishes).[^32][^30][^31]

Global Common Names:
- English: Clown knifefish, clown featherback, ocellated featherback, spotted knifefish.[^30][^33][^34][^32][^29]

Regional & Local Names:
- Mainland Southeast Asia
  - Thailand/Thai: Pla Krai (ปลากราย), Pa tong keua, Pa tong khouay, and related forms; collectively used for featherback/knifefish.[^31][^33][^29]
  - Laos/Lao: Pa Tong Krai.[^30][^31]
  - Cambodia/Central Khmer: Trey Krai (ត្រីក្រៃ), Trey krai, Kaey, Kray.[^35][^33]
  - Vietnam/Vietnamese: Thác lác cườm / thát lát cườm, cá cườm, nàng hai.[^35]
- South Asia (related knifefishes, often confused in trade)
  - India/Bangladesh (for *Chitala chitala* but often generically "knifefish"): Assamese sitawl, Bengali chitol/chital, Hindi and regional languages various local names used for featherback; confusion between *C. ornata* and *C. chitala* is common in markets and aquarium trade.[^36][^37]

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)

Primary Identifying Features:
- Strongly laterally compressed, blade- or knife-shaped body with a long continuous anal fin forming an undulating fringe along the ventral edge; dorsal fin reduced to a small vestigial fin or absent.[^33][^32][^31]
- Small head relative to body with terminal to slightly upturned mouth; large eyes.[^32]
- Caudal region merges with anal fin, giving a continuous fin edge that allows both forward and backward swimming via fin undulations.[^38][^32]

Coloration & Markings (CV cues):
- Base body color silver to gray with darker or brownish back.[^39][^38]
- Distinct row of large, round ocellated (ringed) dark spots along or just above the base of the anal fin—the key field mark distinguishing *C. ornata* from *C. chitala*.[^31][^33]
- No silver or gold transverse bars on dorsum (those bars characterize *C. chitala* instead).[^36][^31]
- Approximate color hexes: Silver flanks #C0C0C0–#D3D3D3, darker back #404040–#4B4B4B, ocelli centers ~#000000 with pale rings ~#D8D8D8.

Sexual Dimorphism:
- External differences subtle; field sexing difficult.
- Males in some populations may show slightly slimmer bodies and longer anal-fin rays; females may appear fuller-bodied when gravid.

Size Metrics:
- Max size: Up to ~100 cm standard length and ~4.95 kg reported.[^40][^32][^31]
- In captivity, often reach 60–90 cm depending on tank size and care.[^38][^40]

Similar Species Look-alikes:
- *Chitala chitala* (Indian featherback): Has series of vertical golden/silver bars on back and smaller, non-ocellated dark spots near tail; lacks the large ocellated spots row of *C. ornata*.[^36]
- Notopterus notopterus (Asian knifefish): Generally more uniform coloration without bold ocelli, and different fin shapes.

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters

Water Type:
- Freshwater, potamodromous species inhabiting rivers, lakes, swamps, and floodplains.[^33][^32][^31]

Geographic Distribution:
- Native: Mekong basin in Laos, Thailand, Cambodia, and Vietnam; Chao Phraya and Mae Klong basins (Thailand).cite:12][^34][^31][^33]
- Introduced: Widely translocated and farmed within Southeast Asia; introduced and established in some non-native regions including parts of the United States (e.g., Florida) and elsewhere through aquarium releases.[^34]

Micro-Habitats:
- Prefers slow-moving rivers, lakes, and swamps with abundant submerged vegetation, woody debris, and cover.[^38][^31][^33]
- Hides among roots, rocks, and dense plants during the day; uses cover to ambush prey.[^32][^38]
- Utilizes inundated forests during seasonal floods for feeding on fish, shrimps, crabs, and insects.[^31]

Depth Profile:
- Common in deeper pools of medium and large rivers and connected lakes, often staying near mid-water or close to cover.[^31]
- During high-water periods, moves into flooded forest at shallow to moderate depths.[^31]

Optimal Water Conditions:
- Temperature: 24–28 °C (approximately 75–82 °F).[^38][^32]
- pH: 6.0–8.0 (slightly acidic to slightly alkaline).[^32]
- Hardness: Soft to moderately hard water.[^38][^32]
- Dissolved oxygen: Prefers well-oxygenated waters but can tolerate moderate fluctuations; generally associated with large floodplain rivers with substantial ventilation.
- Water clarity: Frequently found in tea-colored to turbid waters with submerged structure; not dependent on high clarity.[^33][^31]

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior

Natural Diet:
- Predatory carnivore feeding on smaller fishes, crustaceans (shrimp, crabs), and aquatic insects.[^32][^31]

Feeding Behavior:
- Ambush predator using its flexible body and undulating anal fin to maneuver quietly among cover, then striking quickly at prey.[^38][^31]
- Nocturnal to crepuscular; often rests in hiding spots by day and hunts at night or during dawn/dusk.[^32][^38]

Peak Activity Windows:
- Increased feeding during low-light periods (dawn, dusk, night).
- Seasonal peaks when floodwaters connect rivers to inundated forests and floodplains rich in prey (typically June–October in Mekong system).cite:36]

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear

Best Live/Natural Baits:
- Live small fishes (e.g., river minnows, juvenile cyprinids) presented near structure or in channels linking flooded forest.
- Live or freshly cut shrimp and small crustaceans drifted or lightly weighted.

Best Artificial Lures:
- Soft-plastic swimbaits and jerkbaits worked slowly near the bottom or along submerged timber and vegetation.
- Slim profile minnow plugs and small crankbaits fished close to cover at night or low light.
- In some fisheries, knifefish are more commonly netted or trapped than taken on lures due to nocturnal habits.

Recommended Tackle:
- Rod: 7–8 ft medium to medium-heavy spinning or baitcasting rods.
- Reel: 3000–5000 size spinning reels or equivalent baitcasters with smooth drag.
- Main line: 15–30 lb braided or monofilament line, depending on cover.
- Leader: 20–30 lb monofilament or fluorocarbon to withstand abrasive structures and occasional contact with gill covers.

Community & Regional Techniques:
- Mekong basin fisheries: Knifefish targeted using gill nets, casting nets, and various traps in deep pools and flooded forests; hook-and-line fishing often secondary.[^33][^31]
- Vietnam and Thailand: Fish harvested as part of multi-species river fisheries; common gear includes gill nets, cast nets, and small seines in connecting channels to the Great Lake and floodplains.[^39][^31]
- Recreational and aquarium contexts: Hook-and-line captures usually at night using live baitfish around submerged cover.

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations

Handling Warnings:
- No venomous spines; primary risks include sharp fin rays and potential thrashing of large fish.
- Large individuals are delicate and prone to injury if bent or compressed; support body fully when lifting.

Catch & Release Best Practices:
- Use appropriately heavy tackle to shorten fight time, especially in warm water.
- Keep fish in the water when possible; if lifted, support with both hands under head and tail/caudal region.
- Avoid laying fish on hot, dry surfaces; return gently to water.

Conservation Status & General Regulations:
- IUCN Red List: Least Concern.[^30][^31]
- Regionally important food fish; often no species-specific recreational regulations in native range but may be subject to general gear and effort controls.
- In introduced areas, regulations may treat *C. ornata* as non-native; anglers should check local laws before releasing or transporting fish.[^34]

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile

Commercial Value:
- High regional commercial value as a food fish in Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos; widely farmed and sold fresh or processed.[^35][^39]
- Also significant in ornamental trade as a large, charismatic aquarium species.[^34][^32]

Meat Quality:
- Thin-bodied but flesh is very tender, almost mushy, and filled with many fine bones, spines, and fin rays.[^39]
- Despite bones, valued for unique elastic texture when minced and processed into paste.

Regional Preparation:
- Thailand: Flesh scraped off bones to make fish paste used for fish cakes (tod man pla krai) and fried or grilled pastes; deep-fried clown featherback served with spicy dipping sauces (pla krai thot krathiam).[^39]
- Vietnam: Ground knifefish paste used in soups and various dishes; fried or grilled paste cakes common in Mekong Delta cuisine.[^35][^39]
- Cambodia/Laos/Thailand: Commonly dried, salted, or fermented as part of river-fish preservation traditions.

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)

Q1: How can I visually tell a clown knifefish from the Indian featherback when netting fish at night?
A: Check the flank near the anal-fin base—clown knifefish (*C. ornata*) has a row of large, round, ringed ocellated spots, while Indian featherback (*C. chitala*) has golden bars on the back and only small, non-ringed spots near the tail.[^36][^31]

Q2: What water conditions should I maintain in a holding cage for live knifefish intended for market?
A: Aim for 24–28 °C, pH 6–7.5, good aeration, and moderate flow or water exchange; avoid overcrowding and maintain cover or shade to reduce stress on these nocturnal, cover-oriented fish.[^38][^32]

Q3: When is the best seasonal window to net knifefish in a Mekong tributary?
A: During the rising and high water period when they move into inundated forests and floodplains (often June–October), set gill nets and traps along channels connecting rivers to flooded areas where they hunt fish and crustaceans.[^31]

Q4: Can knifefish be caught reliably on rod and line, or are nets more efficient?
A: They can take live baitfish or slow-worked lures near structure at night, but commercial yields are much higher with gill nets, cast nets, and traps, so most local fishers rely on those gears for efficiency.[^33][^31]

Q5: What is the main culinary reason Southeast Asian processors prefer clown knifefish for fish cakes despite all the bones?
A: Its flesh forms an unusually elastic, cohesive paste after scraping and processing, creating a distinctive bouncy texture in fish cakes and balls that is difficult to replicate with other species.[^35][^39]
