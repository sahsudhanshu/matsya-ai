# Freshwater Eel (*Anguillidae*)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature  
Scientific Name: *Anguilla* (genus) – a representative of the freshwater eels (e.g. European/American eels)【11†L43-L51】【55†L41-L48】. Family: Anguillidae.  
Global Common Names: “Freshwater eel”, “European eel” (*Anguilla anguilla*), “American eel” (*A. rostrata*), and generic market names like “eel” or “eelfish”【55†L39-L43】.  
Regional & Local Names: Includes various local terms for eel. For example, in Southeast Asia some languages use derivations of “eel” (e.g. Tagalog *palos*), and in South Asia terms like “ইল (il)” in Bengali are used for similar eel‐type fish. In Malay/Indonesian, eels are called *ikan belut* (“eel fish”). In other regions eels are typically just called by local words for “eel.” (Note: local names are broadly used and vary by dialect.)  

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)  
【56†embed_image】 Freshwater eels are unmistakable with **slender, elongated, serpentine bodies** tapering to a point【11†L43-L51】. The head is small with a terminal mouth and small eyes. They have **no pelvic fins**, and the long dorsal fin is **continuous** with the anal and tail fins (forming an unbroken soft‐rayed fringe)【11†L43-L51】. Scales are tiny and deeply embedded (skin looks smooth/slimy)【11†L43-L51】. Typical color is mottled brown‐olive on top (#556B2F/olive drab, #4B3621/dark brown), grading to a pale yellow‐cream belly (#F5DEB3/wheat)【11†L47-L51】; mature adults turn a silvery sheen (#C0C0C0/silver) in breeding “silver eel” stage【11†L47-L51】. A faint lateral line often runs along the body.  
- **Primary Identifying Features:**  
  - Long, snake‐like body with continuous dorsal/anal fins and *no* pelvic fins【11†L43-L51】.  
  - Small pectoral fins just behind the gill openings.  
  - Deeply embedded microscopic scales (skin appears scaleless).  
- **Coloration & Markings:** Dark olive/green‐brown dorsum; pale yellowish belly that becomes silvery at maturity【11†L47-L51】. Subtle spotting or mottling on flanks may occur in some species. Juveniles (“glass eels”) are transparent to pale gray.  
- **Sexual Dimorphism:** Not externally obvious beyond size: adult females grow much larger than males【12†L1-L4】. Females often exceed 1.0 m, males rarely >0.5 m【12†L1-L4】. Very large female eels may develop a broad, robust snout (“broadnosed” eel) from heavy feeding【11†L51-L59】. Males mature earlier and stay smaller. No obvious color dimorphism in non-breeding “yellow” eels.  
- **Size Metrics:** Most freshwater eels range 0.4–0.8 m and 250–1000 g【11†L56-L59】. Females commonly ~1.0 m (occasionally up to 1.5 m) and ~4 kg【12†L1-L4】; record sizes ~2 m/8 kg. Legal and trophy sizes depend on region (e.g. EU/US regulations often define minimum catch lengths ~40–60 cm for glass/elver stages).  
- **Similar Species (Look-alikes):** Spiny “freshwater eels” (family Mastacembelidae) resemble true eels but have a distinct row of dorsal spines preceding the soft dorsal fin【14†L207-L211】. Spiny eels have a movable snout and visible spines, while Anguilla eels lack any visible spine and have a smooth head. Moray eels (Muraenidae) live in saltwater and lack pectoral fins (freshwater eels have small pectorals). Burbot (*Lota lota*) is a freshwater cod-like fish with two separate dorsal fins (not eel-like). Gobies/snakeheads are very different-bodied and usually have pelvic fins or different mouths.  
  

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters  
- **Water Type:** Primarily freshwater, but catadromous (live in freshwater but spawn in the sea). Tolerate a range of salinities: adults migrate into brackish/estuarine zones to spawn (marine salinity ~35 ppt for breeding). Juveniles (elvers) enter estuaries and rivers. Can survive in slightly brackish to pure fresh water.  
- **Geographic Distribution:** Cosmopolitan in temperate and tropical zones. European eel (*A. anguilla*) native from Scandinavia through Europe to North Africa; American eel (*A. rostrata*) along Atlantic North America; *A. japonica* in East Asia. Introduced populations exist (e.g. outside native range via human stocking or escape). In general, found in rivers and lakes worldwide (Anguillidae family has ~15–20 species globally)【8†L9-L13】.  
- **Micro-Habitats:** Benthic dwellers in rivers, streams, lakes, swamps and floodplain areas. Hide in cavities: under logs, rocks, burrow in mud, or beneath riprap. Often found in deep holes or pools, especially during the day. Young eels occupy shallow stream margins and submerged vegetation. Some reside in farm ponds or flooded fields during rainy season【11†L43-L51】【24†L17-L20】.  
- **Depth Profile:** Typically shallow (<10 m) in rivers and coastal estuaries. Adults may inhabit deeper river channels or lake bottoms (~10–30 m). During migration, eels occupy near-bottom marine depths (hundreds of meters in oceanic spawning). Usually avoid very shallow or high-current rapids; more common in moderate depths and currents.  
- **Optimal Water Conditions:** Prefer cool to moderate temperatures: about 10–25 °C (temperate species often 13–20 °C)【11†L43-L51】. High dissolved oxygen (>5 mg/L) is important (they slow/metabolize less in well-oxygenated water). pH neutral to slightly acidic/alkaline (pH 6.5–8.0). Clarity is not critical—they thrive in turbid water—but still waters with cover are favored.  

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior  
- **Natural Diet:** Carnivorous opportunists. Juveniles (glass eels/elvers) eat small crustaceans, insect larvae and worms. Adults feed on a wide range of prey: aquatic insects, worms, mollusks, crustaceans (crayfish, shrimps), smaller fish and fish carrion. They are known scavengers as well. In polluted or oxygen-poor habitats, some eels will surface and take terrestrial food (slugs, frogs).  
- **Feeding Behavior:** Primarily nocturnal or crepuscular. Often ambush predators: lie buried in mud or hide in cover by day, then emerge at dusk or night to forage. They glide slowly along bottom scanning for prey by smell. They can probe crevices and silt for buried prey. Large eels can excavate burrows to ambush. Elvers and smaller eels may feed more continuously (diurnal feeding on abundant small prey【44†L1-L4】). Feeding rate increases in warm seasons. They may travel along river margins for feeding at night.  
- **Peak Activity Windows:** Most active from dusk through night and in low-light conditions. Creeping under cover at dawn/dusk. Autumn migration (silver stage) peaks on larger moonlit nights and high tides in estuaries. Barometric changes (falling pressure) sometimes trigger migration and feeding. Elver upstream movements often occur in spring high flows. Eels become less active in cold winter (<10 °C), often burying and entering torpor.  

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear  
- **Best Live/Natural Baits:** Earthworms (nightcrawlers), squid strips, fish strips (herring or sardine), shrimps, or crustacean baits (e.g. crayfish). Lamprey or squid are particularly effective in many regions. Hook bait lightly so eels can take it. Using glowing nightcrawler or garlic-scented baits can attract their keen smell. Bait presented on the bottom (with weight) in holes or near wood cover.  
- **Best Artificial Lures:** Eels are not often caught on artificial lures, but can sometimes be tempted by soft plastic baits (soft-bodied worms or grubs) or small crankbaits on heavy jighead, slowly worked on bottom. Glow-in-the-dark soft plastics or lures that imitate injured fish can trigger strikes. However, live bait rigs (Carolina rigs or drop-shot) are generally more effective than spun lures.  
- **Recommended Tackle:** Heavy to medium-heavy action rod with good backbone to handle large eel without breakage. Use a strong spinning or baitcasting reel with smooth drag. Monofilament or braided line in the 15–30 lb test range (depending on expected size). A shock leader (steel or heavy fluorocarbon 30–80 lb) is recommended because eels have rough skin and may abrade line, and they thrash violently. Use circle or J-hooks size #2–#1/0, or sturdy trebles if legal.  
- **Community & Regional Techniques:** Traditional methods abound. In Europe, fyke nets, eel baskets or poteaux trap-nets (fyke systems) baited with smelly fish are common【11†L124-L132】. In Japan, specialized eel rods (“eel sabiki” style) and glass-eye leads with bait are used. In Asia (India, Bangladesh), fishermen dig up eels by hand or use bamboo traps and conical baskets baited with fish oil. Spearfishing (eel gigging) or snagging under lights is also used. In North America, eel traps or hooked “eel rigs” (big hooks baited with fish) are popular among catfish/eel anglers. In some cultures, eels are jerked from under ledges or nets in shallow nights.  

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations  
- **Handling Warnings:** Eels are slippery and thrash strongly. They have sharp teeth but rarely bite humans unless provoked – still handle with care to avoid cuts. The gill covers are sharp and can inflict cuts. Some tropical eels have mildly toxic slime (cats and dogs should not ingest eel slime). Wear gloves when handling to avoid the slime and potential skin irritation.  
- **Catch & Release Best Practices:** Minimize air exposure; hold eel horizontally with wet hands or wet towel supporting body to avoid internal injury. Unhook with long-nose pliers. Revive by gently sweeping in water so water flows through gills until the eel can swim. Release into calm water near cover. Use circle hooks when possible to reduce deep hooking.  
- **Conservation Status & Regulations:** Most common freshwater eel species are currently not critically endangered, but European and Japanese eels are listed as Endangered/Vulnerable due to overfishing and habitat loss. Check local laws: many regions have size/season limits, or prohibit keeping glass eels/elvers. For example, the EU bans catching juvenile eels. Angling for mature yellow/silver eels is usually allowed with general freshwater fish regulations. Always verify migratory closures: eels often have fall migrations when fishing is restricted.  

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile  
- **Commercial Value:** High value in food markets (especially East Asia). Widely farmed (e.g. Japanese eel aquaculture). Freshwater eels are prized as food (unagi in Japan). They fetch premium prices when live or fresh. Used in restaurants and smoked products. In the aquarium trade, juvenile glass eels are also prized.  
- **Meat Quality:** Eel meat is rich, oily and flavorful with firm, white muscle. Fat content is high (especially in silver stage), making meat buttery but needing careful cooking. Flesh is boneless except a central backbone (filleting yields boneless filets). Pin bones are minimal. Flavor often described as sweet and delicate.  
- **Regional Preparation:** In many Asian cuisines, eels are grilled or broiled with sweet soy sauces (Japanese kabayaki). In Europe (e.g. Germany, Netherlands), eels are smoked or stewed (jellied eels). In China and Southeast Asia, eels are stir-fried, braised, or cooked in soups. In South Asia some communities smoke or curry eel. Local specialties include Japanese unagi, Thai pan-fried eel, and British jellied eels. In northern Europe, eel is often cured or smoked.  

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)  
Q1: **What is the best bait and hook setup for catching large freshwater eels?**  
A: Use natural baits like large nightcrawlers, cut fish or shrimp on a heavy gauge circle or J-hook (sizes around #1/0 to 3/0). Rig it as a bottom bait (sliding sinker or Carolina rig) near cover. A strong braided mainline (20–30 lb) with a heavy fluorocarbon leader (30–60 lb) helps prevent break-offs. The hook should be well-exposed and sturdy – eels swallow baits deeply, so a circle hook often yields secure hooking. Patience is key: wait after nightfall and let eels swallow the bait fully before setting the hook.  

Q2: **How can I distinguish male vs female eels when fishing?**  
A: Sexing live eels is not obvious by appearance. However, in many species females grow much larger. So if you catch a very large eel (close to a meter long), it’s likely female【12†L1-L4】. Males rarely exceed 50–60 cm, so small eels (<60 cm) are often males. Broad, muscular head shape (“broadnosed”) can indicate a well-fed female. But under water, anglers usually can’t tell sex directly.  

Q3: **Are freshwater eels aggressive or dangerous to handle?**  
A: Generally, eels are not aggressive to humans but they can thrash and bite if mishandled. They have sharp gill covers, so wear gloves when handling to avoid cuts. Their skin is extremely slippery – grip with wet cloth or a net. Eels do not have venom; however, their bite can be painful. Overall, treat them carefully and release quickly to minimize stress.  

Q4: **When and where are eels most actively feeding or biting?**  
A: Eels feed mainly at night. The first few hours after dusk or just before dawn are prime times. Look for them in deep holes or near structure (fallen trees, undercut banks). After heavy rains or in high water, eels move more and feed actively. Eels also feed during spring and summer when water warms. During migration in autumn, they may also bite more aggressively just before moving downstream. Moon phase: some anglers note new/full moon periods can trigger eel movements in estuaries.  

Q5: **Are there any legal restrictions on fishing freshwater eels I should know about?**  
A: Yes. Many areas protect juvenile eels (glass eels/elvers) and have size limits. In Europe, landing elvers is banned and you may need a permit for adult eel. Some places forbid removing eels during their autumn migration. Always check local regs: they may require releasing silver (migrating) eels or set closed seasons. If you catch undersize (depending on species and region, often <30–40 cm), release it. Seasons: eels often have fall closure for spawning. Practice catch-and-release outside of allowed harvest times.  

# Glass Perchlet (*Chanda nama*)  

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature  
Scientific Name: *Chanda nama* (F. Hamilton, 1822). Family: Ambassidae (Asiatic glassfishes)【27†L169-L172】.  
Global Common Names: “Elongate glassy perchlet” (English); “Glassy perchlet”, “Glassfish”, or “Nama chanda” in local usage. (Formerly placed as *Ambassis nama* or *Chanda bogoda*.)  
Regional & Local Names: In South Asia this species has several local names. For example, in Assam (India) it is called **Chanda Mach**【24†L7-L10】; among the Bodo people it is **Na Chanda**【24†L7-L10】. In Bengali, it is known as **Nama Chanda** (নামা চান্দা)【38†L101-L110】. It is native to India, Bangladesh, Nepal, Pakistan, Myanmar – in each region simply called “glass perchlet” or local dialect names. (Not present in Philippines/Thailand naturally, though related Ambassidae in SEA are called “tabi” (Tagalog) or “pla salid” (Thai) for glassfish species.)  

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)  
Glass perchlets are small, compressed, laterally flattened fishes. **Body shape:** a somewhat tall, compressed oval profile. The head is pointed; snout moderately long. The first dorsal fin has a few stout spines. Pelvic fins are modified into slender dorsal filamentous threads (as in family Ambassidae). The fin base is short.  
- **Primary Identifying Features:**  
  - **Body:** Deep, laterally compressed (tall), with silvery translucent flanks. Adult size is small (max ~10–11 cm)【24†L7-L10】.  
  - **Fins:** Two dorsal fins; the first has ~5 rigid spines, second dorsal soft‐rayed. Pelvic fins reduced (some Ambassidae have pelvic fins lost or tiny). The anal fin is short. Caudal fin forked. Fin edges often slightly darkened.  
  - **Mouth:** Upward‐tilted, small, with thin jaws – feeding on surface prey.  
- **Coloration & Markings:** Overall glassy silver‐yellow body【38†L101-L110】. The body is pale yellow to silvery (“glassy”), becoming almost transparent in juveniles. Numerous tiny black dots may sprinkle the body【38†L101-L110】. A distinctive shiny **silvery band** runs from behind each gill cover (operculum) to the tail base【38†L101-L104】. Fins (dorsal edges) often have black margins; during breeding males may show orange hues on fins【38†L107-L111】.  
- **Sexual Dimorphism:** Males and females are similar externally, but breeding males develop brighter fin coloration (more orange)【38†L107-L111】. Males may also become more slender, females rounder when gravid. No large size difference; both sexes reach ~10 cm.  
- **Size Metrics:** Grows up to ~11 cm total length【24†L9-L12】; typically encountered at 5–8 cm. Very small by human fishing standards. No legal size limit (usually bycatch).  
- **Similar Species (Look-alikes):** Other Asiatic glassfishes (family Ambassidae) are very similar. For instance, *Parambassis ranga* (Indian glass fish) is more elongated and lacks dorsal spines. Highfin glass perchlets (*Ambassis altipinnis*) have taller dorsal fins. Distinguish *Chanda nama* by its tall, hump‑backed profile and the linear silvery side band【38†L101-L104】. Unlike spiny eels, it has no prominent dorsal spines or elongated snout. Do not confuse with juvenile snakeheads – glass perchlets have a smaller mouth and a “glass” appearance.  

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters  
- **Water Type:** Freshwater and slightly brackish. Found in ponds, streams, ditches and flooded rice paddies. Tolerates low salinity; often enters backwaters and canals.  
- **Geographic Distribution:** Native to South Asia: India (Bengal, Assam, Odisha), Bangladesh, Nepal, Pakistan, Myanmar【27†L169-L172】. Abundant in Ganges-Brahmaputra floodplains. Not known from outside this region (no major introductions).  
- **Micro-Habitats:** Prefers quiet, slow-moving or still waters: canals, oxbow lakes, beels (floodplain lakes), ponds and ponds connected to rivers. Often found near submerged vegetation or woody debris. During the monsoon, abundant in inundated rice fields and wetlands【27†L179-L182】【24†L17-L20】. Common in shallow water (20–50 cm) among weeds. Rare in fast currents or deep clear lakes.  
- **Depth Profile:** Shallow depths (0–1 m) during floods and rains. In dry season, retreats to small permanent ponds or stream margins. Generally near surface (these perchlets can hop/skip to surface insects). Rarely >2–3 m depth.  
- **Optimal Water Conditions:** Tropical climate: warm water 22–30 °C. pH neutral to slightly acidic (6.5–7.5) is typical for South Asian waters. These small fish prefer clear to moderately turbid water. They breathe atmospheric air when needed (ambassador fishes gulp air), so tolerant of low oxygen. Prefer still to slow water with plenty of cover.  

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior  
- **Natural Diet:** Carnivorous, micropredator. Eats mosquito larvae, small aquatic insects (Diptera, mayflies), tiny worms (e.g. tubificid worms), and even scales off other fish【27†L179-L182】【24†L21-L24】 (lepidophagy is noted). As larvae and juveniles, likely planktivorous (zooplankton). Aquarium reports: they take Daphnia, mosquito larvae, small crustaceans.  
- **Feeding Behavior:** Actively forages at dawn and dusk (crepuscular), but will take prey at any time. Often found near surface or midwater, snapping insects off the surface film. Also picks larvae from water. Usually travels in small schools to feed. In captivity they will consume live mosquito larvae eagerly (used in biocontrol)【24†L21-L24】. Not an ambush predator; they swim around vegetation hunting prey.  
- **Peak Activity Windows:** Most active in warm conditions, especially pre-monsoon and monsoon seasons when mosquito larvae abound. Early morning and late afternoon are peak feeding times. They may be less active in cold season. Not strongly tidal or lunar influenced (freshwater species).  

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear  
- **Best Live/Natural Baits:** Very small baits: mosquito larvae, earthworm pieces, small maggots or bloodworms, tiny insects. Fish pieces used too heavy. In practice, they are bycatch when fishing for other species, as specialized fishing for such small fish is uncommon.  
- **Best Artificial Lures:** Tiny lures like micro-jigs, very small spoons or fly patterns (dry flies, wet flies) could attract them. In aquaculture, they accept finely crushed pellet feed. Small floating pellets or cheese niblets can work. But generally not targeted with lures.  
- **Recommended Tackle:** Ultralight gear. Ultra-fine line (1–3 lb test) due to small mouth and size. Small hooks (#16–#12) or micro-jigs. Nano-fishing rigs. Baited traps or fine-mesh dip nets are often used instead of rod.  
- **Community & Regional Techniques:** These fish are not typically targeted by rods. In India/Bangladesh, they are often caught incidentally in seine nets, cast nets, or small dip nets. Fishermen sometimes put out tiny submerged twig traps or bamboo baskets baited with organic waste to catch small glassfish. In aquaculture ponds, they can be collected using hand nets in the rainy season. Some areas use these fish to control mosquito larvae by raising them in ponds.  

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations  
- **Handling Warnings:** Harmless to humans. No spines or toxins. Slender and delicate – handle gently to avoid injuring the fish. Wear gloves if handling many, as slimy coating can be irritating, though not toxic. They bruise easily, so minimal handling is best.  
- **Catch & Release Best Practices:** Keep in water or moist bag until release. Use fine, non-serrated forceps or fingers to remove hooks. Support the fish horizontally in water until it swims away. Avoid dropping from height; these small fish have delicate bones.  
- **Conservation Status & General Regulations:** IUCN status: Least Concern【27†L134-L142】. No special protections (common and abundant locally). No size or bag limits in local fisheries. Because they are small and reproduce quickly, they are not threatened. However, habitat destruction of ponds and pollution can reduce numbers.  

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile  
- **Commercial Value:** Minor. Sold in local markets (in Bangladesh and India) as cheap live bait or small food fish【24†L23-L28】. Occasionally exported (some reports of export from India). No major fisheries; often discards or bycatch used as fertilizer. Aquarium trade: occasionally kept as mosquito-eating aquarium fish.  
- **Meat Quality:** Meat is very fine, delicate, almost gelatinous. Flesh is so small it’s mostly bones; eaten whole in some dishes. Flavor is mild, fishy. Often used whole to make broth. In Assam cuisine they are boiled and mashed into rice. In Bangladesh they are sometimes fermented into chutneys or dried.  
- **Regional Preparation:** Typically eaten in curries or fried with spices in Assam. In some areas they are made into “chutney” by roasting and grinding, or simply boiled with rice. In coastal West Bengal/Bangladesh, they may be cooked with mustard sauce. In Nepal and Pakistan, not commonly eaten. Generally, preparation is rudimentary due to many bones.  

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)  
Q1: **How can I identify an elongate glass perchlet by sight?**  
A: Look for a very small (5–10 cm), silvery-yellow, laterally compressed fish. It has a tall, deep body with a shiny silver stripe from gills to tail【38†L101-L104】. The first dorsal fin spines are visible at the top. The body is semi-transparent (glassy) with tiny dark spots. This distinguishes it from similar transparent fishes (it’s deeper-bodied than a glass minnow).  

Q2: **What bait should I use to catch glass perchlets in rice fields?**  
A: They feed on mosquito larvae and small insects【27†L179-L182】. Using live mosquito larvae or small maggots in a fine mesh bag works well. You can also use crushed rice bran mixed in water to attract them (they will come feed on plankton stirred up). Tiny pieces of worm or local fry pieces will also catch them.  

Q3: **Are glass perchlets aggressive toward other fish?**  
A: They can nip scales off other fishes (lepidophagy)【24†L21-L24】, but they generally keep to themselves when feeding on invertebrates. In mixed tanks they may steal food or pick at weak fish, but they are not harmful in small numbers. They are not predators of large fish, just small insects and zooplankton.  

Q4: **When is the best time to find glass perchlets in local ponds?**  
A: They are most abundant in the rainy (monsoon) season【24†L17-L20】【27†L179-L182】, when ponds and fields flood. Early morning or late afternoon are good times, especially on warm sunny days when insect hatches occur. They often come to the surface to feed on larvae at dusk.  

Q5: **Do any fishing regulations apply to catching glass perchlets?**  
A: No specific regulations exist. They are not a protected species. Because they are small and common, people generally catch them freely. However, since they are often caught incidentally, make sure you are allowed to fish (or net) in that water body. There are no size limits or seasons for this fish.  

# Goby (Family *Gobiidae*)

## 1. Taxonomy & Nomenclature  
Scientific Name: *Gobiidae* (family; e.g. Gobius, Pomatoschistus, Neogobius species). Family: Gobiidae【41†L248-L257】. (For a typical representative, consider the Common Goby *Pomatoschistus microps*.)  
Global Common Names: “Goby” (used for many genera), “gudgeon” (in some European contexts), “dragonet” (for certain subfamilies), and specific names like “round goby” (*Neogobius melanostomus*), “mudskipper” (some amphidromous gobies). Generic “goby” covers thousands of species worldwide【41†L248-L257】.  
Regional & Local Names: Gobies have local names in many languages. Examples: Philippines/Tagalog – “tubigin” or “tikang” (refers to river gobies); India/Hindi – “gompa” (gudgeon) or simply “goby” in English usage; Bangladesh/Bengali – *বেগুনি মাছ* (beguni machh, a fritter, though not specific); Malay/Indonesian – “ikan gobi” or “ketutu” (some gobies); Thai – “pla phuang” (ปลาพวง, used for various small gobies); Japanese – “ドジョウ” (dojo, often for loaches but also some gobies). Because there are so many goby species, local names often refer to particular types (e.g. *Pomatoschistus* as “sand goby” in English).  

## 2. Visual Identification & Anatomy (For Visual/CV Analysis)  
【61†embed_image】 Gobies are typically small, stout fishes with **large, broad heads and tapering bodies**. A defining feature is their **fused pelvic fins**: the two lower fins form a single suction‐cup disc on the belly【41†L297-L304】【60†L194-L199】. They usually have two dorsal fins (anterior spiny, posterior soft‐rayed) and a rounded or slightly forked tail. Eyes are often large and situated high on the head【60†L167-L170】. Many gobies have cryptic coloration. A common pattern is mottled gray‐brown (#808080/gray, #A9A9A9/light gray) with darker blotches (#696969/dim gray) and pale undersides【60†L165-L172】. For example, the Common Goby (*P. microps*) is gray-sandy with darker flecks on back and sides【60†L165-L172】. Some coastal gobies have yellowish or greenish tones; many have a dark spot at the base of the pectoral fin.  
- **Primary Identifying Features:**  
  - **Pelvic fin sucker:** Fused pelvic fins forming a suction cup beneath【41†L297-L304】【60†L194-L199】 (useful to adhere to rocks or glass).  
  - **Fins:** Two distinct dorsal fins (e.g. 6–8 spines in first, 1 spine + 7–10 rays in second【60†L194-L199】). Anal fin origin typically below second dorsal. Pectoral fins broad, used for balance.  
  - **Head/Eyes:** Flat top of head, eyes on upper sides. Many gobies have scaleless head and throat areas【60†L167-L170】. Mouth usually small/moderate.  
- **Coloration & Markings:** Commonly mottled: gray, brown or olive-green (#6B8E23/olive drab, #DEB887/wheat) ground color, with darker blotches or bars across back【60†L167-L170】. A distinct dark “shoulder” blotch at pectoral base is common in many species【60†L167-L170】. Some gobies have bright breeding colors (males may flash yellow or blue). Fin rays may be translucent or lightly tinted. Eyes often golden (#FFD700).  
- **Sexual Dimorphism:** Often subtle. In many species, breeding males become larger and more brightly colored (e.g. vivid patterns, extended fins). Females are usually smaller and drabber. For example, male sand gobies display bright blue spots on fins during mating. Some species have specialized genital papilla shapes. But casual anglers usually see gobies without obvious sexing cues unless breeding behaviors are on display.  
- **Size Metrics:** Most gobies are small: average 5–10 cm. Many common species (e.g. gobies in estuaries) are 4–8 cm. Smallest gobies (e.g. *Pandaka pygmaea*) are under 2 cm【41†L249-L257】. Large gobies (rare) can reach 30+ cm (e.g. giant goby *Gobius cobitis*). Typical angling bycatch gobies ~3–7 cm.  
- **Similar Species (Look-alikes):** Blennies (family Blenniidae) resemble gobies superficially (small, benthic), but blennies have one long continuous dorsal fin (no separate dorsal fins) and no pelvic sucker. Snakeheads have elongate bodies and distinct fin patterns. Sand lance and sticklebacks differ in fin count. A key feature: gobies’ fused ventral sucker【41†L297-L304】. Also, non-Gobiidae fish called “gobies” (like goby-list catfishes or goby eels) belong to other families – look for morphological clues (gobies have ctenoid scales, some notches in skull anatomy).  

## 3. Habitat & Environmental Parameters  
- **Water Type:** Many gobies are coastal/marine, but numerous genera inhabit fresh and brackish waters【41†L309-L317】. Some (*Oxyeleotris*, *Gobio*, *Glossogobius*) are true freshwater. Gobies in this profile include estuarine to freshwater species (tolerant of salinity 0–35 ppt). Gobies can live in stable freshwater streams (sand goby *Pomatoschistus*), tidal creeks, or up to full-strength seawater (reef gobies).  
- **Geographic Distribution:** Global, in tropical and temperate regions【41†L309-L317】. In Asia: widespread in rivers/estuaries (e.g. *Glossogobius* sp. in Indo-Pak; mudskippers and sleeper gobies in Indo-Pacific). In Europe: native gobies along coasts and some freshwater rivers. North America: some *Eucyclogobius* on Pacific coast, round goby (*Neogobius melanostomus*) invasive Great Lakes. Africa: *Oxyeleotris marmoratus* in SE Asia, others. Basically, gobies inhabit almost all coasts and many inland waters worldwide.  
- **Micro-Habitats:** Benthic, bottom-dwelling. Typical shelters: sand burrows, rock crevices, coral rubble, aquatic vegetation. Many dig shallow pits or occupy shrimp-burrow symbioses. In rivers, found along muddy/sandy substrates and among submerged plants. In tidal zones, gobies endure fluctuating water and can be found in tide pools, salt marsh puddles, and mangrove creeks. Some gobies climb tidal flat rivulets at low tide.  
- **Depth Profile:** Usually shallow coastal waters. Many gobies live intertidally to a few meters deep (reef gobies often 1–10 m; round goby down to 80 m). Freshwater gobies inhabit near-surface rivers and streams (<10 m). Some deep-sea gobies exist, but typical anglers see those in <50 m. The common goby (*P. microps*) is often <1 m deep (tide pools to 10 m)【60†L183-L191】.  
- **Optimal Water Conditions:** Depends on species: marine gobies prefer stable salinity (~30–35 ppt) and tropical to temperate temps (15–28 °C). Freshwater gobies thrive at neutral pH (~7–8) and warm temps (~20–30 °C), often tolerating low oxygen by gulping air. Many gobies are hardy; estuarine species endure salinity swings and muddy water. They prefer moderate clarity (some are sensitive to extreme turbidity).  

## 4. Diet, Foraging & Behavior  
- **Natural Diet:** Carnivorous bottom-feeders. Small gobies eat planktonic crustaceans (copepods, amphipods), insect larvae, tiny mollusks and worms【60†L165-L172】. Larger gobies consume small fish fry and benthic invertebrates. Mudskipper-like gobies will even eat algae, insects, crabs in mud. Some specialized gobies (e.g. neon gobies) clean parasites off larger fish. Generally, prey size is small (<1 cm).  
- **Feeding Behavior:** Many gobies perch on the bottom and dart out to snatch passing prey. They often hover by burrows or hold station with pectorals. Gobies are active feeders during daylight, especially in shallow waters. Some species feed at night on plankton. In tide pools, they may feed on stranded worms and crustaceans. Gobies sometimes bury in substrate and ambush prey.  
- **Peak Activity Windows:** Typically diurnal feeders (active daylight). Peak feeding often occurs with rising tide or during slack high tide, bringing in more prey. Many fresh/brackish gobies spawn in spring, with increased movement. Moon phase: not strongly tied, though some local fishermen note more gobies at full moon (unknown mechanism). In low-pressure weather (storms), bottom feeders like gobies may feed more actively.  

## 5. Tactical Fishing Guide & Gear  
- **Best Live/Natural Baits:** Very small baits. Tiny shrimp, pieces of mussel, worms (bloodworms, sandworms), or micro-fish scraps. In sea fishing, artificial crabs or shrimp imitations also work. For freshwater gobies (gudgeons), small maggots or earthworm chunks. In practice, gobies are often caught accidentally on small bait aimed at bigger species (they scavenge bottom baits).  
- **Best Artificial Lures:** Gobies often hit micro-lures. Popular lures include 2–3 inch soft plastic jigs (sand eel imitators), small spoons (1–2 cm), or micro minnow plugs. In saltwater, tiny bucktail jigs or sabiki rigs with shrimp flies can attract them. Fly anglers use small streamers or sand eel flies. Retrieval should be slow and near bottom. For freshwater gobies, ultralight spinners with single hooks and sticky plastic tails work.  
- **Recommended Tackle:** Ultralight spinning rods (UL action) with small reels spooled with 2–6 lb line. In saltwater, light tackle up to 8 lb works. Braided line (4–8 lb) is helpful for sensitivity with small lures. Use long-nose pliers for unhooking. Because gobies are small and often caught as bycatch, finesse gear is key. Some anglers even use small nets or bait traps (mini-fyke nets) to catch gobies in shallow waters or pools.  
- **Community & Regional Techniques:** In Europe, gobies are rarely targeted with rod; often collected by tidepooling or push nets by anglers and shore diggers. In Asia (Philippines), gobies (mudskippers/gudgeons) are often trapped with baited baskets or speared in shallow tidal creeks. In the U.S., the invasive round goby is fished with bottom rigs (using goby mimics as bait for bass). Nets: Seine nets and cast nets in estuaries will catch gobies by the dozen. Some fishermen use small minnow traps baited with bread or cat food.  

## 6. Handling, Safety & Regulations  
- **Handling Warnings:** Gobies are generally harmless. Be cautious of their spines: many gobies have first dorsal spines or gill cover spines that can puncture skin. Use pliers or gloves to avoid these. If released, gobies stick to wet hands due to suction cup, handle gently. Their suction disc can stick to gear. They can bite if handled by mouth (unlikely), but the bites are minor.  
- **Catch & Release Best Practices:** Handle with wet hands or a wet cloth to avoid removing slime coat. Do not lift by tail. If deeply hooked (though small hooks usually catch lip/mouth), consider cutting line. Release quickly as they are sensitive to air exposure. Gobies often go into shock; holding them upright in water until they swim off is good. Use barbless or micro barbless hooks if practicing C&R to minimize injury.  
- **Conservation Status & General Regulations:** As a family, gobies are vast; most are listed as Least Concern. Some endemic or rare species may be protected locally (e.g. certain freshwater gobies in endangered habitats). Invasive species (round goby) often have no protections. There are generally no specific bag limits or size limits for common gobies. However, some states require live bait to be non-invasive. E.g., catch-and-release of round goby is often mandated. Always check if your target goby is invasive in the area (some regions forbid release).  

## 7. Culinary & Commercial Profile  
- **Commercial Value:** Usually low. Many gobies are too small to sell; however, large gobies (e.g. *Acentrogobius* spp. in Asia) are eaten locally. In Europe, gobies (called *gobie* in Italy/France) are sometimes sold as *tétraodon gobius* or used as live bait. The invasive round goby now is used as bait or ground fish meal. Certain Asian gobies are consumed: e.g. marble goby (actually Eleotridae) is farmed, though not a true goby. Overall, gobies have limited direct market value except niche uses (bait fish or novelty table fish).  
- **Meat Quality:** Small-bodied (often only grams), so not much meat per fish. The meat is white and flaky but bony. Flavor is mild and reminiscent of other small whitefish. Fishermen say goby meat is okay when removed from bones (used in soups). They have many fine rib bones. Fat content is low.  
- **Regional Preparation:** In Southeast Asia, small gobies (e.g. rice-field gobies) are sometimes fried whole in batter (similar to salted eggplant fritters) or made into fish cakes. In Vietnam, goby hotpot (“lẩu cá kèo”) is a delicacy【52†L99-L102】【52†L128-L133】. In Mediterranean countries (Venice, Adriatic), goby (“gò”) are traditionally cleaned for soups or risottos【50†L100-L108】. They are often fried or used to make broth because of the many bones【50†L100-L108】. In East Africa, some communities smoke or fry small gobies for fish curry. In all cases, gobies are a modest table fish, usually combined with stronger flavors (spices, sauces) or ground into other preparations.  

## 8. Simulated Fisherman Q&A (For RAG Context)  
Q1: **What bottom baits do gobies find irresistible?**  
A: Tiny crustacean baits are best. Small pieces of shrimp, crab, or clam on a tiny hook will attract gobies. In freshwater, maggots or earthworm pieces are effective. Gobies scavenge along the bottom, so placing the bait right on sandy or muddy substrate is key. Brightly colored small jigs (white or chartreuse micro-plastics) also mimic prey. Essentially any small natural bait on a light jig head will trigger gobies.  

Q2: **I keep catching gobies in my estuary traps; can I release or must I keep them?**  
A: In most regions gobies are not regulated, so you can release them if you like (catch-and-release fine). If it’s the invasive round goby, regulations often encourage removal and non-release (some areas allow keeping to reduce population). If you release native gobies, simply shake them gently out of the trap back into the water. They survive well if handled carefully. Always check local invasive species rules.  

Q3: **How do I tell a goby from a small blenny or sculpin?**  
A: Look under the fish for the pelvic fin “sucker” – gobies have their pelvic fins fused into a suction cup【41†L297-L304】. Blennies have separate pelvic fins and usually one long dorsal fin. Also, gobies typically have two distinct dorsal fins (blennies one continuous fin). Gobies often have rounder heads and bigger eyes on the sides. Blennies often have cirri (small tentacles) on their heads. Sculpins lack a suction disc and have larger pectoral fins and a distinct angular head.  

Q4: **What weather or tide conditions make gobies more likely to bite?**  
A: Gobies bite well in normal daylight conditions, especially during rising tide or high tide in estuaries. At night some species (e.g. round goby) feed actively around lights. Overcast or rainy days might spur feeding because of more invertebrate activity. In very low tides, gobies concentrate in pools and bite readily. They are less affected by lunar phase than migratory fish, but a big tide usually stirs up food.  

Q5: **Are there any size or bag limits for gobies where I fish?**  
A: Almost never. Gobies are generally either not managed or are considered “trash” fish. No size limits typically. In some places, if they are invasive (like round goby in the Great Lakes), they encourage removal and say not to release them. In most freshwater settings, you’re free to catch and keep or release gobies at will. They are not usually protected species.
